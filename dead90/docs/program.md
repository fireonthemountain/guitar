# Ninety Days to the Jam
*A Grateful Dead guitar program — beginner to light intermediate*

**The goal: an open mic on night 90 — three songs, rhythm and lead, standing, confident.**

The set you are building: **Ripple** (strummed verses with banjo-roll texture and fills), **Friend of the Devil** (opens with its descending intro run), and a **closer — Sugaree or Uncle John's Band** (double-stop fills and an 8-bar solo break).

## How to read tab
Each line is a string; the top line is the thinnest (high e). Numbers are frets, 0 means play the string open, x means don't play that string. Read left to right.

## How to use this file
Every day is a checkbox — tick it when the session is done. Each session is ~40 minutes. Log one line per day in the Practice Log at the bottom; that's your history.

## The five strum patterns
Rotate one per day through every Phase 1 chord session (day 1 = #1, day 2 = #2, … repeating).

```
count:         1  &  2  &  3  &  4  &
1 Foundation   D     D     D     D
2 Backbeat     D     D  U  D     D  U
3 Campfire     D     D  U     U  D  U
4 Boom-chick   B     D     B     D
5 The Chop     D     x     D     x
```

B = pick just the bass string of the chord. x = muted slap — relax the fret hand and strum the thunk.
Foundation = steady · Backbeat = bounce · Campfire = the classic · Boom-chick = Dead country · The Chop = groove.

## Milestones
- **Day 30** — loop G–C–D with the strum pattern for 3 straight minutes, no stopping
- **Day 60** — Ripple and Friend of the Devil back to back, from memory, recorded, with at least one lead fill in each
- **Day 90** — the open mic: three songs, standing, fills throughout, solo break in the closer

# Phase 1 — Foundation (days 1–30)

## Week 1: Home Base: Mixolydian (days 1–7)

### Lesson
A scale is a family of notes that belong together. The major scale is the do-re-mi sound you already know. Mixolydian is a major scale with exactly one change: the 7th note is lowered by one fret. That single flatted 7th (the "b7") turns sunny into sunny-with-dust — the Grateful Dead's home sound. Jerry spent a whole career inside this scale.

This week you learn E Mixolydian in open position: E, F#, G#, A, B, C#, D. In the tab below, each line is a string (top line = thinnest string), numbers are frets, and 0 means play the string open. Read left to right, low notes first.

**E Mixolydian — two octaves, open position**
```
e|---------------------------0--
B|----------------------2-3-----
G|----------------1-2-4---------
D|----------0-2-4---------------
A|----0-2-4---------------------
E|-0-2-4------------------------
```
**This week's chords — E, A, D (x = don't play that string)**
```
      E   A   D
e|----0---0---2--
B|----0---2---3--
G|----1---2---2--
D|----2---2---0--
A|----2---0---x--
E|----0---x---x--
```

**Key terms**
- **Mixolydian** — A major scale with the 7th note lowered one fret
- **b7** — That lowered 7th — the note that makes it sound like the Dead
- **Open position** — The first few frets, using open strings

### This week's session template (~40 min)
- **Warmup (5 min)** — Chromatic 1-2-3-4 drill: frets 1-2-3-4 on every string, one finger per fret, down-up picking, 60 bpm
- **Technique (20 min)** — Work today's focus — the numbered steps above
- **Chords (15 min)** — Open E, A, and D — shapes are drawn just below. Run the one-minute change test: switch E→A as many times as you can in 60 seconds, log the count in your notes. Then A→D. Beat yesterday's number.

**Week 1 chords — E, A, D (x = don't play that string)**
```
      E   A   D
e|----0---0---2--
B|----0---2---3--
G|----1---2---2--
D|----2---2---0--
A|----2---0---x--
E|----0---x---x--
```

### Days

- [ ] **Day 1 — Meet E Mixolydian — first octave, open position**
  1. Play just the first octave of the lesson tab: low E string 0-2-4, A string 0-2-4, D string 0-2.
  2. One finger per fret: index takes fret 2, ring takes fret 4.
  3. Ten slow, clean passes at 60 bpm. Clean beats fast, every time.

- [ ] **Day 2 — Full two octaves, up and back down — 70 bpm**
  1. Play the whole lesson tab going up, then reverse it coming down.
  2. No pauses at string crossings — that's the entire game today.
  3. If a crossing stumbles, loop just those two strings until it doesn't.

- [ ] **Day 3 — Say every note name out loud as you play**
  1. E, F#, G#, A, B, C#, D — speak each one as you fret it.
  2. It feels slow. It's wiring the fretboard into your brain.
  3. Bonus round: name them coming back down too.

- [ ] **Day 4 — Sequence in 3s: E-F#-G#, F#-G#-A, G#-A-B…**
  1. Play three notes, restart one scale note higher, repeat all the way up.
  2. Keep strict down-up picking throughout.
  3. This exact pattern shows up in half of Jerry's runs.

  **The first four groups of the sequence**
```
A|-----------0------0-2----0-2-4-
E|-0-2-4---2-4----4---------------
```

- [ ] **Day 5 — Play the mode over an E7 drone — hear the b7**
  1. Search YouTube for an "E7 drone" or "E Mixolydian backing track."
  2. Play the scale slowly over it and pause on the D note each pass.
  3. That slightly bluesy rub is the b7. That's the sound you're chasing.

- [ ] **Day 6 — Move the shape: A Mixolydian and D Mixolydian**
  1. Same fingering logic starting from the open A string: A, B, C#, D, E, F#, G.
  2. Then start from the open D string: D, E, F#, G, A, B, C.
  3. The pattern travels. That's the fretboard's biggest secret.

  **A Mixolydian — two octaves, open position**
```
e|--------------------------0-2-3-5-
B|--------------------2-3-----------
G|--------------0-2-4---------------
D|--------0-2-4---------------------
A|--0-2-4---------------------------
```

  **D Mixolydian — one octave, open position**
```
B|----------------1-3-
G|--------0-2-4-------
D|--0-2-4-------------
```

- [ ] **Day 7 — Free play over an E7 vamp — scale notes only**
  1. Backing track on. No rules except: stay inside the scale.
  2. Play short phrases with rests between them, not endless streams.
  3. Hit a sour note? Slide one fret either way — you're never far from home.

## Week 2: Major Pentatonic & the Jerry Box (days 8–14)

### Lesson
Pentatonic means five notes. Take the major scale, remove the 4th and 7th, and what's left is the major pentatonic — nearly impossible to make sound bad, and the skeleton under most Garcia solos. The Dead live in the key of B a lot, so that's where we learn it.

The "box" below sits between frets 4 and 7. Your root notes (B) are at fret 7 on both E strings and fret 4 on the G string. Learn where the roots are and the box becomes a map instead of a shape.

**B major pentatonic — box 1 (frets 4–7)**
```
e|-4-7-
B|-4-7-
G|-4-6-
D|-4-6-
A|-4-6-
E|-4-7-
```
**This week's chords — G, C, D**
```
      G   C   D
e|----3---0---2--
B|----0---1---3--
G|----0---0---2--
D|----0---2---0--
A|----2---3---x--
E|----3---x---x--
```

**Key terms**
- **Pentatonic** — A five-note scale — the major scale minus its 4th and 7th
- **Box** — A scale pattern that stays in one spot on the neck
- **Root** — The note the key is named after — your home base

### This week's session template (~40 min)
- **Warmup (5 min)** — Chromatic 1-2-3-4 drill: frets 1-2-3-4 on every string, one finger per fret, down-up picking, 60 bpm
- **Technique (20 min)** — Work today's focus — the numbered steps above
- **Chords (15 min)** — Open G, C, and D — shapes below. One-minute change tests: G→C, then C→D. Then loop G–C–G–D slowly, four downstrums per chord, no gaps between changes.

**Week 2 chords — G, C, D**
```
      G   C   D
e|----3---0---2--
B|----0---1---3--
G|----0---0---2--
D|----0---2---0--
A|----2---3---x--
E|----3---x---x--
```

### Days

- [ ] **Day 8 — B major pentatonic, box 1 — memorize it cold**
  1. Play the lesson box bottom string to top and back, 60 bpm.
  2. Find every B as you pass it: fret 7 low E, fret 4 G, fret 7 high e.
  3. Ten passes, then try it with your eyes closed.

- [ ] **Day 9 — Connect box 1 to the next box with slides**
  1. Here's box 2 — it overlaps box 1 and sits a couple frets higher.
  2. Go up through box 1, slide up the B string from 7 to 9, come back down through box 2.
  3. Smooth beats fast. The slide should be silent work.

  **B major pentatonic — box 2 (frets 6–9)**
```
e|-7-9-
B|-7-9-
G|-6-8-
D|-6-9-
A|-6-9-
E|-7-9-
```

- [ ] **Day 10 — Add the 4 and b7 — penta melts into Mixolydian**
  1. Add two notes to the box: E at fret 5 on the B string, and A at fret 7 on the D string.
  2. Pentatonic is the safe zone; those two notes are the color.
  3. Alternate: one phrase plain, one phrase with color.

  **The two color notes inside box 1**
```
B|--5--   E, the 4th
D|--7--   A, the b7
```

- [ ] **Day 11 — Ascend one box, descend the next**
  1. Up box 1, shift up, come down box 2 — one continuous line.
  2. Metronome at 70. Don't rush the position shift.
  3. Then run the whole trip in reverse.

- [ ] **Day 12 — Target the 3rd (D#) every time the chord lands**
  1. Loop a B chord vamp. Every time a bar starts, land on D# — fret 4, B string.
  2. Approach it from above one pass, from below the next.
  3. The 3rd is the sweetest note in any chord. Feel why.

- [ ] **Day 13 — Call and response: 2-bar question, 2-bar answer**
  1. Play a short phrase that ends on any note EXCEPT B. That's the question.
  2. Answer it with a phrase that ends on B.
  3. This is the moment solos become conversations.

- [ ] **Day 14 — Free play over a B vamp — pentatonic only**
  1. Five minutes minimum, backing track on.
  2. The limit is the freedom: five notes force real phrasing.
  3. Reuse your best question-and-answer pair from yesterday.

## Week 3: CAGED Triads Up the Neck (days 15–21)

### Lesson
A triad is the smallest possible chord: three notes — the root, the 3rd, and the 5th. The CAGED idea says the five open chord shapes you already know (C, A, G, E, D) repeat up the neck, which means every chord lives in five different places. Jerry's rhythm playing — and a lot of his lead playing — leans on these small triads instead of big barre chords.

Start with A major on just the top three strings. Below are the same three notes (A, C#, E) in three different neighborhoods. Strum only the three thinnest strings.

**A major triads — top three strings, three positions**
```
e|--5----9----12--
B|--5----10---14--
G|--6----9----14--
```
**This week's chords — Em and Am join G, C, D**
```
      Em  Am
e|----0---0--
B|----0---1--
G|----0---2--
D|----2---2--
A|----2---0--
E|----0---x--
```

**Key terms**
- **Triad** — A three-note chord: root, 3rd, 5th
- **CAGED** — The five chord shapes that tile the whole neck
- **Voicing** — One particular arrangement of a chord's notes

- **Chords (15 min)** — Add Em and Am — shapes below. Loop G–Em–C–D, four beats each. That's the folk engine under half the songbook. Keep the strumming plain; the changes are the workout.

**Week 3 chords — G, C, D, plus Em and Am**

```
A|----2---3---x---2---0--
```

### Days

- [ ] **Day 15 — A major in three places — learn the lesson triads**
  1. Play each of the three tab shapes slowly, top three strings only.
  2. Say "A major" out loud each time — same chord, new address.
  3. One relaxed downstroke per shape. Let it ring.

- [ ] **Day 16 — The middle shape is movable — find D, G, and A**
  1. Take the middle lesson shape and slide it: the fret you land on sets the key.
  2. Find D major, G major, and A major with it.
  3. Movable shapes are the whole neck unlocking at once.

- [ ] **Day 17 — Connect two shapes on one chord**
  1. Pick one chord and play it in the lowest lesson shape, then the next one up.
  2. Walk between them slowly until the jump feels like one motion.
  3. Slow is fine. Smooth is the goal.

- [ ] **Day 18 — Play the Ripple progression with tiny triads**
  1. Look up the chords to Ripple (it lives around G, C, A, and D).
  2. Comp the whole verse using only top-three-string triads. No barre chords today.
  3. Hear how much lighter it sounds? That's the Dead's rhythm texture.

- [ ] **Day 19 — Arpeggiate every triad — one note at a time**
  1. Same shapes, but pick each string separately and let the notes ring together.
  2. Strict down-up picking.
  3. This is rhythm playing and lead playing shaking hands.

- [ ] **Day 20 — Triads through G–C–D with minimal movement**
  1. Use these three triads — they live within a few frets of each other.
  2. Change chords by moving one or two fingers, never jumping.
  3. Loop the progression until the changes happen by themselves.

  **G, C, and D triads — top three strings, close together**

- [ ] **Day 21 — Song run: Uncle John's Band, triads only**

  2. Hum the melody over your own playing.
  3. Record 30 seconds on your phone and listen back.

## Week 4: The Right Hand (days 22–28)

### Lesson
Tone and groove live in the right hand. Alternate picking means strict down-up-down-up, no exceptions, even when you cross strings. Hybrid picking means you keep holding the pick but pluck extra strings with your middle and ring fingers — Jerry's country-flavored snap comes straight from this.

The banjo roll is a repeating three-string pattern borrowed from bluegrass (Garcia was a banjo player first). Hold an open G chord and loop the pattern below: the pick strikes the D string, your middle finger plucks the B, your ring finger plucks the high e. Let every note ring into the next.

**Banjo roll on open G — pick, middle, ring, repeat**
```
e|------0------0------0---
B|----0------0------0-----
D|--0------0------0-------
```
**This week's strum pattern — over the G–C–D loop**
```
count:  1    2  &    &  4  &
strum:  D    D  U    U  D  U
(keep the arm swinging on the silent beats)
```

**Key terms**
- **Alternate picking** — Strict down-up-down-up pick strokes
- **Hybrid picking** — Pick plus bare fingers plucking at the same time
- **Roll** — A repeating fingerpicking pattern that rings like a banjo

  3. Four clean passes across all six strings.

- [ ] **Day 23 — String crossing in 4s, strict alternation**
  1. Play your E Mixolydian scale, never breaking the down-up order.
  2. The hard part is an up-stroke landing on a fatter string. That's normal.
  3. Slow down until the crossings are silent.

- [ ] **Day 24 — Hybrid picking: pick plus middle finger**
  1. Pick the open A string, then instantly pluck fret 2 on the B string with your bare middle finger.

- [ ] **Day 26 — Accent shifting: lean on 2 and 4**
  1. Run yesterday's roll, but play beats 2 and 4 louder than 1 and 3.

- [ ] **Day 27 — Everything from this week, +10 bpm**

## Week 5: Bends, Vibrato & Slides (days 29–35)

### Lesson
A bend pushes the string sideways to raise its pitch — it's the guitar imitating the human voice. A whole-step bend must land exactly on the pitch two frets higher; your ear is the referee, not your hands. Vibrato is a small, steady wobble that keeps a held note alive instead of letting it die.

Check your bend intonation like this: play fret 9 on the G string and memorize that pitch. Then bend fret 7 until the two match. Always bend with three fingers — the ring finger frets the note while middle and index push behind it for strength.

**The pitch-check bend — target first, then bend to match**
```
G|--9----7b9----7b9--
```
**This week's chords — E7, A7, B7, plus the 12-bar loop**
```
      E7  A7  B7
e|----0---0---2--
B|----3---2---0--
G|----1---0---2--
D|----0---2---1--
A|----2---0---2--
E|----0---x---x--

| E7 | E7 | E7 | E7 |
| A7 | A7 | E7 | E7 |
| B7 | A7 | E7 | E7 |
```

**Key terms**
- **Whole step** — A two-fret distance in pitch
- **Oblique bend** — Bending one string while another rings unbent
- **Vibrato** — A controlled, repeating wobble on a held note

### This week's session template (~40 min)

- **Chords (15 min)** — Dominant 7ths: E7, A7, B7 — shapes below. Loop a 12-bar in E: E7×4 bars, A7×2, E7×2, B7×1, A7×1, E7×2. This is the blues floor under the whole Dead catalog.

```
B|----3---2---0--
G|----1---0---2--
D|----0---2---1--
A|----2---0---2--
E|----0---x---x--

| E7 | E7 | E7 | E7 |
| A7 | A7 | E7 | E7 |
| B7 | A7 | E7 | E7 |
```

### Days

- [ ] **Day 29 — Whole-step bends, pitch-checked**
  1. Run the lesson drill: hear fret 9, then bend fret 7 up to match it.
  2. Three fingers behind every bend — ring bends, middle and index help push.
  3. Ten bends. Be brutally honest about the pitch.

- [ ] **Day 30 — Half-step bend and release, slow motion**
  - 🔺 MILESTONE — Checkpoint 1: loop G–C–D with the strum pattern for 3 straight minutes, no stopping. That's a song's worth of endurance.
  1. Bend fret 7 up just one fret's worth — check it against fret 8.
  2. Then lower it back down as slowly and evenly as you raised it.
  3. The release should be silent: no scrapes, no wobble.

- [ ] **Day 31 — Vibrato: slow and wide, then narrow**
  1. Hold fret 7 on the B string and rock your wrist to wobble the pitch.
  2. Exaggerate first — big slow waves — then gradually tighten.
  3. Sync it to the metronome: one full wave per beat.

- [ ] **Day 32 — Slide into chord tones from below**
  1. Pick one fret below your target note and slide in without re-picking.
  2. Target the 3rds and 5ths of a B chord.
  3. Slides are cheap expression. Spend freely.

- [ ] **Day 33 — Bend and hold while picking a second string**
  1. Bend the G string at fret 7 up a whole step, hold it there, and pick fret 5 on the B string.
  2. This oblique move is pure pedal steel — pure Jerry.
  3. It hurts today. It's gorgeous in a month.

  1. Hum any four-note melody — anything.

# Phase 2 — Vocabulary (days 31–60)

## Week 6: Chromatic Color (days 36–42)

### Lesson
Chromatic notes are the in-between notes — anything one fret outside your scale. Played carelessly they sound wrong; played on purpose they create motion and pull. The one rule: chromatic notes pass through, they never park. Always land on a chord tone.

The approach note is the easiest version: play the note one fret below your target, then resolve up onto it. The enclosure surrounds the target — one note above, one below, then land. Below, an enclosure that circles and lands on B.

**Enclosure landing on B — above, below, target**
```
G|--5--3--4--
```

**Key terms**
- **Chromatic** — A note one fret outside the scale
- **Approach note** — The note one fret below a target, resolving up
- **Enclosure** — Circling a target: above, below, then land

### This week's session template (~40 min)
- **Warmup (5 min)** — 60 seconds of the banjo roll on G, then E Mixolydian two octaves up and down, twice
- **Vocabulary (15 min)** — Work today's focus — the numbered steps above
- **Application (10 min)** — Loop a B vamp (record 2 minutes of comping, or find a backing track) and use today's idea over it, slow tempo first
- **Repertoire (10 min)** — Ripple start to finish, singing or humming. Now add lead: end each vocal line with a small fill — slide into the 3rd of the next chord (your Week 5 skill). Strummer AND soloist, four bars at a time.

### Days

- [ ] **Day 36 — Approach every chord tone from one fret below**
  1. Loop a B chord. Before each chord tone (B, D#, F#), play the fret below it first.
  2. Approach note on the weak beat, target note on the strong beat.
  3. Instant jazz-country flavor. Notice how the target now shines.

- [ ] **Day 37 — Enclosures: above, below, land**
  1. Run the lesson tab: C, then A#, landing on B.
  2. Build the same move around D# and around F#.
  3. An enclosure is a spotlight pointed at the target note.

- [ ] **Day 38 — Chromatic walks between pentatonic boxes**
  1. Travel from box 1 to box 2 on one string, hitting every fret in between.
  2. Time the walk so you arrive on a scale note exactly on beat one.
  3. Passing through — never parking.

- [ ] **Day 39 — The ascending chromatic run into the root**
  1. Walk up into B: frets 2, 3, 4 on the G string (A, A#, B).
  2. End the run exactly on beat one.
  3. A classic Garcia sentence-ender. File it away.

  **The chromatic walk-up — land B on beat one**
```
G|--2--3--4--
     A  A#  B
```

- [ ] **Day 40 — Passing tones inside the scale**
  1. Play Mixolydian phrases, but fill one whole-step gap per phrase with its in-between fret.
  2. Keep passing tones quick and light — grace notes, not destinations.
  3. The scale is the road; these are the gravel shoulders.

- [ ] **Day 41 — One chromatic idea per phrase — no more**
  1. Solo over a B vamp. Each phrase gets exactly one approach, enclosure, or walk.
  2. Restraint is what makes them shine.
  3. Overuse turns color into seasickness.

- [ ] **Day 42 — Free play — count your chromatic moves**
  1. Five-minute jam. Tally every chromatic device you use.
  2. Aim for 8–12 total: present, not constant.
  3. Note which one is starting to feel like yours.

## Week 7: Arpeggio Targeting (days 43–49)

### Lesson
An arpeggio is a chord played one note at a time. When you solo, arpeggios are the safety rails: chord tones are the notes that always sound right over that chord, no matter what. ## Week 9: Double Stops (days 57–63)

**Key terms**
- **Double stop** — Two notes played at the same time

## Week 11: Melodic Development (days 71–77)

### Lesson
A motif is a tiny idea — three to five notes. Great solos aren't a parade of unrelated licks; they take one idea and grow it: repeat it, move it higher, flip it upside down, stretch its rhythm. Listeners follow a solo the way they follow a sentence — through repetition and variation.

Jerry's other secret is simpler: he starts from the vocal melody and decorates it. When you're lost mid-solo, play the melody. It always works, and nobody plays it enough.

**Motif development — one idea, three treatments**
```

```

**Key terms**
- **Motif** — A tiny musical idea, 3–5 notes
